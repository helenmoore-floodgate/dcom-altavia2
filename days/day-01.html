A gentle opener. The Plose gondola lifts you from St. Andrä to Kreuztal (≈2,050 m), saving 1,500 m of road. Trail 7 traverses to Rifugio Plose (2,447 m) and one of the great panoramas of the Eastern Alps — the Odle ahead, glaciers behind.

Then trail 4 winds down through dwarf pine and pasture toward the Würzjoch country, easing onto the broad shelf of the Halslhütte. A short first day by design: a sun terrace, a slice of Kaiserschmarrn, legs saved for tomorrow.

1Plose gondola top start
ViewPlose / Kreuztal-Valcroce (2,050 m)

The classic curtain-raiser of the Alta Via 2. From Brixen — Bressanone in Italian, the oldest town in Tyrol and the gateway to the whole high route — the Plose cabinovia, a six-seater gondola running since 1985, lifts you up the wooded eastern flank of the city's local mountain to the Kreuztal-Valcroce station at about 2,050 m. The Plose massif itself is not yet true Dolomite; it sits in the Lüsen (Lüsner) range and is built of older, gentler rock, which is exactly why its summits — the Gabler (2,576 m), the Pfannspitze (2,547 m) and the Telegraph (2,486 m) — roll up in grassy, rounded domes rather than the sheer pale towers you have come to see. That contrast is the point: the Plose is the green grandstand from which the real Dolomites are revealed.

The gondola is a deliberate piece of trail kindness. The original AV2 began far below in the Eisack (Isarco) valley and ground its way up more than 1,500 vertical metres of forest before the views even started; the lift dissolves nearly all of that brutal first-day climb and deposits you, in minutes, already at the altitude where the meadows open and the air turns thin and bright. Step out of the cabin and the whole northern rampart of the Dolomites swings into view across alpine pasture grazed by cattle whose bells you will hear long before the cars in the valley fade.

2Rifugio Plose panorama
HutPlosehütte / Rifugio Città di Bressanone (2,447 m)

A short climb up the broad trail from the gondola gains the Plosehütte — Rifugio Città di Bressanone alla Plose in Italian — a handsome stone refuge perched at about 2,447 m near the Telegraph summit, just below the high point of the Plose. This is the true trailhead of the Alta Via 2, the "Dolomite High Route of the Legends," the point from which the long traverse to Feltre officially begins, and it is one of the great panoramic terraces of South Tyrol.

The hut is genuinely old, one of the venerable refuges of the eastern Alps. The decision to build it was taken in 1884 by the Brixen/Bressanone section of the German and Austrian Alpine Club (DÖAV); construction by volunteers began in May 1887, and it was inaugurated with a grand ceremony on 7 November 1887, with a first enlargement following as early as 1907. Its name tells the rest of the South Tyrolean story: after the First World War, when this German-speaking land was annexed to Italy, the hut was confiscated during the Fascist period and in 1923 handed to the Italian Alpine Club (CAI) of Bressanone, which took over its management the following year and runs it to this day. That tangle of two names and two clubs on a single building is the human history of the whole region in miniature.

3Coffee at the Schatzerhütte ☕
Coffee stopSchatzerhütte (1,984 m)

A short way along the Plose traverse, the family-run Schatzerhütte makes a relaxed early stop — coffee and cake, or a speck platter if you fancy an early lunch (warm kitchen ~12:00–16:00). It sits just off the high line, an easy few minutes away.

4First sight of Sass de Putia
SummitPeitlerkofel / Sass de Putia (2,875 m)

As the trail curls south toward the Würzjoch, the Peitlerkofel rears up alone against the sky, and you understand at once why it has been a landmark for centuries. Standing apart from every neighbouring massif, its pale dolomite walls climb to 2,875 m at the Großer Peitler, with a second summit, the Kleiner Peitler (2,813 m), set off across a deep notch in the ridge. It is the most recognisable peak of the northern Dolomites — a lone, fortress-like tower rising straight out of green pasture — and a mountain you will circle, photograph and orient yourself by for the first days of the AV2.

The mountain wears three names, the trilingual signature of this corner of South Tyrol where German, Italian and the ancient Ladin language all live side by side: Peitlerkofel in German, Sass de Putia in Italian, and Sas de Pütia in Ladin (sas simply meaning "stone" or "rock" in Ladin, the Rhaeto-Romance tongue still spoken in the surrounding valleys). It rises on the watershed between Val Badia to the east and the Villnöss/Funes valley to the west, at the far northern edge of the Puez-Geisler (Puez-Odle) Nature Park — the protected wild heart you are now entering.

5Sunlit drop to the family hut
HutHalslhütte (1,866 m), Würzjoch / Passo delle Erbe

An easy, sunlit descent on trail no. 8A brings the day to a gentle close at the Halslhütte, a cheerful family inn at about 1,866 m on the Würzjoch — Passo delle Erbe in Italian, the "pass of the herbs" — a high road col set directly at the foot of the Peitlerkofel. After the long traverse from the Plose, the trail simply tips down through meadow and larch to the pass, and the hut appears with the great pale tower of the Peitlerkofel filling the sky behind it: an easy, generous first day's end.

The Würzjoch itself is a place of some character. At roughly 2,000 m it links the Villnöss/Funes valley with Val Badia, carrying a quiet mountain road over the saddle, and its name — pass of herbs — points to the rich alpine flora of these limestone-edged meadows. This is the very col of the witch legends that haunt the Peitlerkofel: the spot where, in local tradition, the storms were brewed on full-moon nights, and where the Ladin Bal dles Stries festival is staged each July. Standing here at dusk, with the tower above going from grey to rose, it is not hard to feel why a mountain like this gathered so many stories.

The Plose, Peitlerkofel & Würzjoch — gateway to the AV2

Historical things to know

Above Brixen rises the Plose, a broad grassy massif (its summit, the Gabler, reaches about 2,562 m) served by a cable car from St. Andrä. The Plose is the practical starting point of the Alta Via 2: from the top of the gondola, walkers head south and east across alpine pasture toward the first great rock peaks. This high country between the Plose, the Würzjoch and the Peitlerkofel marks the transition from the soft grasslands of the South Tyrolean foreland into the abrupt pale-limestone towers that define the range proper.

The Peitlerkofel (Ladin Sass de Pütia, 2,875 m) is a strikingly isolated double-summit massif, generally regarded as the northernmost true peak of the Dolomites. It stands on the boundary between Val Badia and Val di Funes (Villnöss), rising in dramatic solitude above meadows. Reaching the Peitlerscharte (Forcella di Putia), the saddle at its foot, is the point at which AV2 walkers enter the Puez-Odle Nature Park; from the saddle a via ferrata gives an optional ascent of the summit.

The Würzjoch (Passo delle Erbe, about 2,004 m) is the road pass that crosses this country, linking Brixen with the Ladin Val Badia and separating the Peitlerkofel from the Lüsen mountains to the north. The pass and its pastures are heavily Ladin in character and form the start of the classic Peitlerkofel circuit. For AV2 trekkers it is a key waypoint between the Plose and the Puez-Odle high plateau.

The hub hut for this section is the Schlüterhütte (Rifugio Genova), built in 1898 with funds donated by the German industrialist Franz Schlüter, near the Forcella Poma between the Peitlerkofel and the Odle (Geisler) group. After the First World War and the transfer of South Tyrol to Italy, the hut — like many German-Austrian Alpine Club refuges — passed to the CAI, acquiring its Italian name Rifugio Genova. It is the standard waypoint where the route enters the heart of the Puez-Odle park.

💡 Fun fact

You're starting in South Tyrol (Südtirol / Alto Adige), one of the few corners of Italy with three official languages — German, Italian and Ladin. German speakers are the majority up here, which is why tonight you sleep in a Hütte, not a rifugio.
